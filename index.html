Research Achievements

  • (2024) Master's Thesis: Novel Deep Learning Architectures for DNA Basecalling published in TU Delft repository (link pending as thesis is under embargo)
    • Basecalling is a task where a neural network turns a one-dimensional signal into a DNA nucleotide sequence
    • Contributed the discovery of the impact of distillation on state-of-the-art architectures, which improved throughput at the high-accuracy regime by up to 85 percent.
    • Also contributed three novel architectures:
      • DenseBaseConv, a convolution-based architecture modification of state-of-the-art Bonito which, at equivalent optimization levels and at high-throughput regimes, improved throughput by up to 35% at equal accuracy, significantly improving the speed-accuracy trade-off in DNA sequencing analysis
      • Bonito-GRU, a model based on state-of-the-art Bonito where we showed that LSTM layers can be replaced with GRU layers without accuracy losses, reducing encoder FLOPs by 25%
      • SACall-CoT, a model based on state of the art in transformer-based basecallers SACall where the architecture is modified to include the chain-of-thought mechanism showing a small improvement in accuracy at matched throughput
    • Research supervised by Distinguished Researcher Peter Hofstee (IBM) and Associate Professor Zaid Al Ars (TU Delft), focusing on accelerating genomics research by reducing computational bottlenecks (basecalling is a large source of cost for long-read sequencing, which will be commonplace in 10 years)
    • Conducted comprehensive analysis of sequence-to-sequence models' performance in basecalling, including transformers, RNNs, and state-space models, including
  • (2024) Research publication: on power networks optimization and efficiency published in Elsevier's International Journal of Electrical Power & Energy Systems
    • Born out of a GNN-focused project, I worked with two PhD students focusing on ML for electrical grids. Our model predicts variables in a power grid using a GNN trained using a physics-informed loss to shorten the time needed per simulation.
    • I was responsible for setting up the training pipeline, initial design decisions and an interpretability study of the model.
  • (2022) Bachelor's Thesis: First public implementation of DeepMind's "Fictitious co-play (FCP)" method, presented to top-ranked (at TU Delft) ML researcher Frans Oliehoek (Grade: 9/10)
  • (2023) Research Paper: Developed novel RL blindness technique under ex-Oxford researcher Wendelin Böhmer
    • Started as part of a course and on the basis of professor Böhmer's idea, we work on a novel task, observation robustness, where an agent's state is temporarily inaccessible, but the agent must keep solving a task (here, finding its way through a maze).
    • We frame this as an open-loop control task, and contribute the first open-loop loss applied to online deep reinforcement learning. Our agent not only successfully becomes robust, but generalises to longer open-loop timespans than it was trained on.
    • I mainly contributed to codebase design, experimental setup, and final experiment decisions, as well as the writing of the paper.
  • (2022) Research Project: Achieved 9.5 grade on quantum computing project implementing variational eigensolver on QuTech's 5-Qubit quantum computer
    • As part of the Quantum Science and Information Minor at the prestigious QuTech insitute within the Factulty of Applied Sciences and under professor Menno Veldhorst who was part of the first team to ever show universal quantum logic in silicon, I was part of a 5-person team implementing a variational eigensolver from research running for the first time on a TU Delft quantum computer.
    • We were praised for the high quality of the results despite being a team of computer scientists with only one physics student, as well as the quality of our analysis of the results.
    • As this was done within the challenging Quantum Science and Information minor, which is not recommended for Computer Science students due to its difficulty, I learned a lot about the physicist's way of solving and framing challenging problems.
  • (2023) Research blog post: Demonstrated competitive gesture recognition model training using pre-trained DINOv2 embeddings
    • This was part of the Computer Vision course of my Msc degree.
    • Given the semantic nature of the DINOv2 model's loss, we wanted to know if enough positional information was present to use it to improve on the state of the art in a small benchmark gesture recognition dataset. This was achieved successfully.
    • I was responsible for designing the experimental setup, generating results and the subsequent write-up.
